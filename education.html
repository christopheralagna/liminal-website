ANTI-CAPITALIST EDUCATION

Our Educational Perspectives

There are a few theoretical concepts which inform the way we approach education. Select the topics below to learn more:

Events as Education

Events are at the core of Liminal’s praxis.

All events hosted by Liminal can be understood as diversified forms of anti-capitalist education, where the ongoing mergence of activity and theory will take place. Below are some examples:

  • potlucks // food accessibility
  • clothing swaps // LGBTQIA and subversive fashion
  • film critiques // cultural hegemony
  • jazz listening // black history + erasure
  • art making // resistance
  • community celebration // community issues

We believe that events which prioritize active community discussion / involvement bring about a learning environment which is immediately applicable, accessible, mutually supportive, enticing, resists gate-keeping, resists competitive individualism and feels collectively empowering.

Community Education

In an attempt to cultivate modes of learning which are communally-based // and mutually-driven, many programs at Liminal will consist of classes and workshops led by members of our community.

This initiative is in direct opposition to the hierarchical methods of corporate and state-sponsored education - which is notoriously inaccessible, unaffordable, hyper-competitive, and guided by state and corporate interests.

We believe that folks don’t need to have professional certification, or go into debt, in order to learn, to teach, and to grow. By putting an emphasis on mutual community support, community education can provide new perspectives on teaching, more accessible modes of learning, more relevant // vital topics of discussion, more intimate environments for bonding with others, newfound appreciation of those around us, the cultivation of self-confidence and personal empowerment, and so on.

Political Education

One of Liminal’s many anti-capitalist objectives is to help spread teachings about oppression and resistance strategies. Therefore, much of our programming will be based around the tradition of ‘political education’ (explained below). This initiative will take form as a series of ongoing courses, book clubs, panel discussions, and various other educational programs and events.

Issues with Mainstream Education
You might ask: ‘Aren’t state-recognized forms of education sufficient?’ It’s easy to think that universities and other expensive educational programs are the only places where people learn valuable information, and where ideas get developed that really matter. However, ideas which are commonly considered ‘important’ have been determined by the interests of corporations, the interests of the state, and the interests of those who otherwise hold the most social, cultural, and political power.

Privatized and state-sponsored education are capitalistic initiatives: corporations profit and nations benefit, both from the skilled labor of graduated students, and also from the cultural indoctrination that results from prescribed educational topics/materials which bolster national ideals. These institutions are fundamentally opposed to liberatory and anti-capitalistic education. Therefore, revolutionaries have historically organized anti-institutional and grassroots ways of arming the working class with knowledge that can demystify the complicated nature of oppression; one such iteration is known as ‘political education’.

Political Education as a Solution
Political education (originally ‘popular education’) is all of the following: a tradition, a method of teaching (pedagogy), and a commitment to spreading knowledge of oppression rooted in capitalism. The tradition of political education stems from the work of Paulo Friere, who was an educator and philosopher in Brazil during the mid 20th century. He asserted that education ought to be ‘a practice of freedom’, and that liberatory ideas must be taught through the mutual guidance of the people, rather than from middle-upper class individuals with professional status or extensive academic recognition. In following the legacy of Friere's teachings, many other organizations - such as The People's Forum - have created free or affordable political education programs.

In a university setting, you are not likely to learn about oppression, let alone how to resist oppression. An anti-capitalist curriculum would include subjects like: community and conflict, political economics, work and welfare, cultural hegemony and ideological control, political power and state repression, histories of colonialism, and/or global development. Such a curriculum might also include the intellectual tradition of black internationalism/radicalism: an especially important topic which would put contemporary issues of racism into a meaningful historical context; thus illuminating centuries of capitalistic racial exploitation (racial capitalism). The integration of these fields of study are vital to truly understanding how the world works in terms of power.

Liminal will need plenty of help from our community in our pursuit of developing anti-capitalist curriculums. Please reach out if you would like to work with us to plan and organize classes // events // and other initiatives of this sort!
